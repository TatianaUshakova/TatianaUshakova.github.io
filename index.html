Tatiana Ushakova

I am a physicist and ML engineer. I work on physical AI, building tools for human-AI integration, automating research with AI, I am interested in robotics and alignment research.

Background

I conducted research on integrable models of quantum field theory in A. Belavin's group (one of the founding fathers of integrable models) in Moscow, and did my PhD in astrophysics at Johns Hopkins University studying atmospheric escape.

In the middle of my PhD, after GPT's appearance, I decided I'm much more interested in applying AI to real-world problems than in conducting theoretical physics research (at least at this point in time). I transitioned to industry and currently work in ML/AI.

Current & Recent Work

Recently I've been working as a leading ML algorithm developer for radar perception in a startup building a system to detect weapons in public places like school zones and airports. I was responsible for the technical development of the product: determining a robust method of product design, identifying reliable weapons proxies from radar data in noisy cluttered backgrounds, finding features from physical principles that allow stable detection with low false-negative rates, choosing ML architectures, analyzing system tradeoffs and prioritizing directions by physical and algorithmic feasibility, writing minimal simulations and minimal experimental designs that generate the most information per time spent, doing mathematical modeling, and writing physics-based processing code.

Earlier I worked as an ML engineer in wearables, where we tried to track users' emotional state from wearable data. I developed ML models to process wearable sensor data, extract meaningful features, and validate the scientific approach. Developing main approach involved designing ways to connect multiple imperfect noisy datasets to extract complex relations, and developing statistical methods to estimate how well the approach worked given limitations of datasets and imperfect correlations in features.

Projects

In my free time I read research across various areas and build fun tools. Recently I've been working with the SO-arm 101 robotics arm on a couple of small projects, including a minimal version of a robotics cooker, and on more research side exploring whether the Karpathy-style autoresearch loop can extend to robotics, which is a nontrivial task because it needs tight iteration loops and can't be easily parallelized, but there's some hope for meaningful output from partial automation.

I've also been building small tools for fun, including:

  • Theoretical-physics-translate-LLM: Coming from a theoretical physics background, many radar-related books were annoying to read because they contained too much text description and weren't dense enough on formulas, meaning I spent much more time reading than I would on a few dense pages of PDF. I'm building a tool that converts any-topic PDFs into a style natural to a theoretical physicist. The natural extension would be a tool that takes any arXiv paper and rewrites it to another arXiv paper in the domain language of the reader, eliminating cognitive barriers to reading cross-domain publications.
  • Time-spent-advisor: Most productivity tools tell you the breakdown of time spent on apps but give very little actionable information on what to do with it. I spent a lot of time analyzing my time use manually, asking "what exactly did I do ('submitted a refund for a trip', not 'answered emails'), what was the purpose of this action, and is it the best way to spend my time?", which gave me a lot of useful information for improving my productivity. Now I'm building an app that does the same automatically.

These - and maybe a few more - will be posted in the projects section soon.
